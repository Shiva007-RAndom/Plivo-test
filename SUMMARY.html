01 · What was built

Pipeline

Each candidate pause is scored using only the audio strictly before it — the ~2.5 seconds of speech leading in, never the pause itself or anything after. From that window, eight causal features are extracted: trailing energy level and its decay rate into the pause, the pitch (F0) level and its slope over the last voiced stretch, a final-syllable lengthening ratio, and two features normalizing energy/pitch against that speaker's own running baseline. A RandomForestClassifier (100 trees, depth 3) trained separately per language turns those eight numbers into p_eot, the probability a given pause is really the end of the turn.

The deployment policy simulated by the scorer: wait a fixed delay after a pause starts; if p_eot has crossed a threshold by then, respond; otherwise wait out a 1.6s timeout. The scorer sweeps every (threshold, delay) pair and reports the fastest one that keeps false interruptions at or below 5% of turns — that single number, mean response delay, is what's being minimized throughout.

04 · Diagnosis

Where it still fails

The textbook cue for endpointing — pitch falls at the end of a sentence — turns out to be close to a coin flip in this data. Across all 248 English pauses: pauses with clearly falling pitch were actually holds 52% of the time; pauses with flat or rising pitch were true ends only 39% of the time, barely above the 40% base rate. Spectrogram inspection of the worst misclassifications confirmed it directly — genuine end-of-turn pauses with rising pitch, and mid-list pauses with the falling-pitch pattern that "should" mean an ending.

Every one of the eight features shows this same heavy class overlap when checked individually. That's consistent with the observed plateau: once feature engineering and model search both stopped moving the number (see runs 6 and 8), the honest conclusion was that prosody alone is close to its ceiling for this distinction — the reliable version of "was that actually finished?" is usually lexical, not acoustic.

06 · Why it beats the status quo

Net result

English is a clear, verified win: 30% lower response delay than a silence timer, at the same 5%-interruption safety budget, measured by cross-validated evaluation rather than the in-sample numbers the starter train.py would have produced. Hindi is a more honest story than a bigger number would have been: response delay is tied with the naive baseline because the data itself sets an ~800ms floor regardless of model quality — but AUC climbed from 0.501 (chance) to 0.691, meaning the model is discriminating real signal that this particular dataset's pause-duration distribution just doesn't reward. A system that quietly reported a fake win on Hindi, or kept spending effort chasing a number the data can't give, would be worse than one that measured the floor and said so.
